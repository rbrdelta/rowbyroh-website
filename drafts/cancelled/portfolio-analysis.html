Portfolio Analysis

Quantitative finance applied to personal portfolio management

A concentrated personal portfolio analyzed through Modern Portfolio Theory, Monte Carlo simulation, and a Bayesian updating framework. The goal: maximize long-term upside while constraining annual losses, using the same rigor typically reserved for institutional portfolios.

01

The Problem

The portfolio had grown significantly over five years — strong returns driven by early conviction in a few positions. But conviction had created extreme concentration. One position represented over 60% of total value. The next largest sector block pushed effective exposure past 90% in correlated assets.

Gut feel said "it's working, don't touch it." The math said something different.

Concentration at a glance
  • Largest single position: 66% of portfolio value
  • Top 3 holdings: 95% of portfolio value
  • HHI concentration score: 8,221 — anything above 2,500 is "concentrated"
  • Zero exposure to healthcare, consumer staples, utilities, fixed income, international developed markets
It wasn't a portfolio — it was a leveraged bet with decorative diversification.

The Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) sums the squares of each position's portfolio weight. A fully diversified 20-stock portfolio scores around 500. A portfolio with one dominant position scores in the thousands.

02

Prior Work

This wasn't the first time I'd done this analysis. A financial modeling capstone project had covered the full Modern Portfolio Theory toolkit: efficient frontier construction, Sharpe ratio optimization, CAPM regression, and Monte Carlo simulation. That project used a two-asset portfolio (total bond index + S&P 500 index) to demonstrate the math.

Capstone findings on a two-asset portfolio
  • Optimal allocation: ~68% bonds / ~32% equities — maximized Sharpe ratio at 0.48
  • The optimal mix returned less than pure equities but with dramatically less risk
  • Monte Carlo projection on $5M: optimal mix range of $273K vs. single-stock range of $2.9M
  • Risk-adjusted returns matter more than raw returns — the math is unambiguous

The capstone proved the theory. Now the question was whether I'd apply my own conclusions to my own money — or keep doing what felt good.

03

Methodology

The analysis has three layers, each building on the last. Correlation analysis reveals hidden concentration. Monte Carlo simulation quantifies the range of outcomes. The Bayesian framework turns a one-time analysis into a decision system that improves over time.

04

Layer 1: Correlation and Covariance

Position sizing alone doesn't capture risk. Two stocks at 10% each could behave like one 20% position if they're highly correlated. The semiconductor holdings in this portfolio — spread across chip designers, foundries, and equipment makers — looked diversified by company but moved together in practice.

Correlation matrix: n holdings → 171 unique pairs Covariance matrix: magnitude of co-movement → feeds portfolio variance calculation → feeds efficient frontier optimization

The "diversified" 10% of the portfolio that wasn't the dominant position was itself 62% semiconductors. In a tech downturn, the non-concentrated portion would correlate with the concentrated portion. Effective diversification was near zero.

05

Layer 2: Monte Carlo Simulation

Historical averages tell you the expected outcome. Monte Carlo tells you the range of outcomes and how likely each one is. The simulation generates thousands of possible portfolio paths by sampling from the historical return distributions of each holding, respecting the correlations between them.

Objective: maximize E[5-year portfolio return]
Constraint: P(annual loss > 20%) < 10%
Simulations: 10,000 scenarios over 5-year horizon
Output: worst case, mean, best case, 95% confidence interval

The constraint is the important part. Unconstrained optimization just tells you to concentrate in the highest-returning asset — which is exactly the current portfolio. Adding the loss constraint forces the optimizer to find allocations where the upside remains high but the downside is bounded.

06

Layer 3: Bayesian Updating

A one-time optimization is a snapshot. Markets change. Companies change. The analysis that's correct in January may be wrong by June. The Bayesian framework turns static analysis into a living decision system.

For each holding, define a hypothesis: "This company will compound at X% annually over the next five years." Assign a prior probability based on historical data, current fundamentals, and competitive position. Then update that probability as new evidence arrives.

Prior (historical + forward analysis) ↓ quarterly earnings, competitive shifts, regulatory changes Posterior (updated via Bayes' theorem) ↓ crosses threshold? Decision: adjust position size ↓ Next prior = yesterday's posterior
07

Update Frequency

How often to update matters. Too frequent and you're reacting to noise. Too infrequent and you miss regime changes. The statistically sound answer for a five-year investment horizon: quarterly, with event-driven overrides.

Daily and weekly price movements are 80–90% noise. Monthly returns are still majority noise. Quarterly earnings are high-signal. 20 quarterly updates over a five-year horizon provides enough data points while preventing overreaction.

Update trigger Signal quality Action
Quarterly earnings High Full Bayesian update on all holdings
Material event High Override update on affected holding
Analyst rating change Low Ignore
Daily price movement Noise Ignore
08

What It Revealed

The analysis confirmed what the HHI score suggested: the portfolio was dramatically off the efficient frontier. High returns had masked high risk. The expected return was strong, but the tail risk — the probability of catastrophic annual loss — was far outside the acceptable constraint.

The rebalancing path was clear: reduce the dominant position from 66% to under 10%. Diversify across uncorrelated sectors. Add fixed income exposure. The optimizer showed that expected returns dropped modestly while downside risk dropped dramatically — a favorable trade at any reasonable risk preference.

Key findings
  • Portfolio Sharpe ratio improved significantly after rebalancing toward the efficient frontier
  • Adding negatively correlated assets (bonds, defensive sectors) reduced portfolio volatility more than proportionally
  • The "diversified" semiconductor holdings were 0.7+ correlated — effective diversification was illusory
  • Bayesian framework revealed that quarterly updating with event overrides is optimal for a 5-year horizon
09

The System, Not the Answer

The specific allocations matter less than the framework. Markets shift. Correlations change. New opportunities appear. What persists is the decision system: quantify concentration, simulate outcomes under constraints, update beliefs with evidence, act on thresholds rather than feelings.

The capstone proved the math works in theory. This analysis proved I'd actually use it on my own capital. The Bayesian framework means I don't need to be right once — I need a system that gets less wrong over time.
