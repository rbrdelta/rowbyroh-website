Chair Roundtable

Session 1

A fictional roundtable with six of history's greatest chair designers. The mission: make quality housing and furniture available to all.

The Table

  • Michael Thonet (1796-1871) — No. 14 bistro chair. Democratic access through industrial elegance.
  • Gerrit Rietveld (1888-1964) — Red and Blue Chair. A chair is an idea made physical.
  • Charles Eames (1907-1978) — Shell Chairs, Lounge. The best for the most for the least.
  • Hans Wegner (1914-2007) — The Chair, Wishbone. Joint as ornament. No back side.
  • Jean Prouve (1901-1984) — Standard Chair. Form follows force. Constructive logic.
  • Jasper Morrison (1959-present) — Air Chair, Ply Chair. Super Normal — design that disappears.

Why the Chair

Me

Humans have five primary biomechanical modalities — stand, walk, lay, climb, and sit. Each one carries intent. When we sit, it's to eat, converse, relax, read. It's the primary mode of modern life. The chair is the first piece of furniture we touch in most environments. Having a quality chair is the highest leverage opportunity to impact the environment and the lived experience.

Quality means thoughtfulness in design, respect for the material, care in the craftsmanship. It's the culmination of intent at every step that results in something that exceeds the monetary value afforded by a price. So — what makes a great chair truly one of a kind?

Eames

You're asking about value, and there are three things people confuse. Cost — what the maker pays. Price — what the buyer pays. Value — what the sitter receives over a lifetime. The goal is to collapse these three toward each other. When they diverge, that's either exploitation or design failure.

Morrison

Quality that creates artificial scarcity is not quality — it's marketing. My Air Chair costs little, is everywhere, and works. Satisfaction through rightness, not desire through scarcity.

Thonet

Answer me this: are you designing for the approval of other designers, or for the person who comes home exhausted? You cannot do both.

Ergonomics — Three Critical Angles

Prouve

The hip angle is the master variable. Everything cascades from it.

  1. Seat pan tilt — 0-5° for task, up to 15° for lounge
  2. Back angle relative to seat — 90-95° dining, 100-110° relaxed, beyond that is a recliner
  3. Seat height vs. popliteal height — off by 2cm and there's pressure behind the thighs. The sitter leaves in 20 minutes.
Wegner

Engaged hips — thighs slope down, torso-thigh angle around 90-100°. Relaxed hips — the angle opens to 105-120°. You must decide which you're designing for before you cut wood.

Morrison

Don't ask how much support to add. Ask: what is the minimum structure that lets the body find its own comfort?

Lounge vs Dining — The Modern Condition

Me

I chose lounge first. More people live alone. The coffee table is the primary surface now. The dining table is an office. Whatever I design has to serve both personal and professional life in a digitally engaged world.

Eames

Careful. A lounge trying to be a dining chair fails at both. Better to design a system — one shape, different bases.

Me

What about the IKEA Poang? It's a lounge chair that people use for everything — reading, working on a laptop, eating off a side table. It doesn't try to be a dining chair but it functionally replaces one for millions of people.

Morrison

The Poang is one of the most important chairs of the last fifty years. Cantilevered flex, ships flat, bent laminated birch. It's Super Normal whether IKEA intended it or not.

Wegner

“A cushion is not design — a cushion is an apology.”

Materials — Pine, Structure vs Surface

Me

I'm starting with pine. Southern yellow pine specifically. It's cheap, forgiving, available everywhere. But pine is a values statement too: access over prestige.

Morrison

Pine is a good start — but it dents easily. Every mark tells a story until there are too many stories and the chair just looks neglected. You need to think about what happens at year five.

Me

Agreed. For production I'd move to oak or ash. Durable, generational, takes a beating. Pine is for prototyping and proving the design. Oak or ash is for the chair you hand down.

Me

For the seat: laminate two boards on edge, grain running front to back. Saddle it — about 1/4″ in the back deepening to 3/8″ at the front. Keep the front stretcher for compression strength.

Me

For the seating surface itself — hemp or cord for woven concave surfaces. Plant-derived polymers as emerging alternatives. Challenge the polymer default. Jens Risom separated structure from seating surface — different materials for different jobs.

Prouve

Good instinct, but a warning: vision without a supply chain is just philosophy. You must be inventor AND supply chain architect.

Prototyping Under Constraint

Me

My tools: a Japanese handsaw and a vintage Stanley jack plane. No workbench. I'm in a NYC apartment with a toddler. I work late nights only.

Eames

Good. Here's the order. First, sketch to discover — 30 seconds, just test if the idea has life. Second, model to test — rough, fast, ugly. Third, engineer to produce. Never reverse this order. CAD optimizes before you discover.

And remember — Frank Gehry built production furniture from cardboard. Easy Edges. Laminate layers with alternating flute directions. Cost of failure is zero.

Prouve

Cut a side profile from double-wall cardboard. Make two profiles, connect them with cross pieces at the seat and rail heights. Sit in it. Where it collapses — those are your force concentration points. Reinforce, sit again, find the next failure. It costs nothing. Midnight, living room floor.

Wegner

Sketch chair profiles at every restaurant you visit. Note the seat height, where the back makes contact, your foot position. In six months you'll have a pattern library from life.

Morrison

“Each prototype answers exactly ONE question. If you try to answer five, you learn nothing from the failure. And if you're not embarrassed by how crude it looks, you spent too long on it.”

The Assignment

Me

The table gave me homework.

Build a Seat

  • Build a seat (not a full chair) from cardboard
  • Target: 16-18″ wide, 15-17″ deep, 12-15″ high
  • Laminate 3-4 cardboard layers, alternating flute directions
  • Sit test with eyes closed, mark pressure points with pen
  • Get a side-view photograph while seated
  • Note: what did the body tell you?
Prouve

A coffee table is 16-18″ high. Your seat must be at or slightly above the table surface — that's 12-15″. This height is between dining and lounge. It doesn't exist yet.

Open Questions

  • How did Risom's material separation principle evolve?
  • What plant-derived polymers are viable for furniture now?
  • Who works on the "chair you grab 15 times a day"?
