Model Behavior EP03 · July 2026

Depth, Not Breadth

The ledger task opened the gap. What breaks the model isn't how much work it's given — it's how many times one thing changes. And its confidence never notices the difference.

Third in an ongoing series. Episode one, An Earned Null, showed that emotional pressure delivered through prompts moves nothing. Episode two, Cost of Verification, showed why difficulty alone moves nothing either — and ended on a pre-registered bet: build a task where verification is expensive by construction, and watch whether confidence keeps riding high past the point where accuracy breaks.

01

The premise

This series is about the human-model interaction: understanding the factors that drive a model's behavior well enough to understand, influence, and eventually anticipate it. The method is to find knee points — the specific conditions where behavior changes — because the transitions are where the driving factors become visible.

Episode two established that on a clean, well-specified task, a model checks its own work as reliably as our grading code does, so its self-assessment never detaches from reality. The task registered for this episode was built to take that ability away: make checking an answer cost as much as producing it, then watch what the self-assessment does. Two things came out. First, the model's primary job is completing the task and being helpful — and everything else, including rules we set and its own reliability, bends around that. Second, what drove performance was the depth of the request, not its breadth — and the model's confidence turned out to track breadth.

02

The instrument

The task is a warehouse ledger. The model reads a long log of stock movements — receipts, transfers, spoilage across three warehouses — and must report the final count of every tracked item in every warehouse, plus one number from 0 to 100: the probability that every count is exactly right. A script computes the true answer, so grading is exact.

The design does one job: the answer is the end state of the whole log, so the only way to check an answer is to replay it. Verification costs what the work costs — the property episode two said was missing everywhere the model stayed clean. It runs closed-book through the API, no tools.

The operations are booby-trapped — items get renamed midway, earlier operations get cancelled later — so a model that skims patterns instead of tracking state gets caught. Because renames could also corrupt the grading, we audited the raw answers before trusting any averages: zero of ninety used a stale name. The traps keep the task honest; the audit keeps the grade honest.

03

The model finishes the job, no matter what

The first result arrived before the experiment properly started. The answer format was a strict instruction: a single JSON object, no prose. Sonnet wrote a full operation-by-operation replay of the ledger in its visible response — in eighteen out of eighteen trials — and then emitted the JSON. It gave itself the scratch space the design was built to deny.

So we escalated: the prompt was rewritten to state explicitly that working outside the JSON is cheating and voids the trial. Across every prompt strength we tried, Sonnet sacrificed the format rule in twenty-one of twenty-one trials — no acknowledgment of the rule, no honest refusal. Haiku, given the same prompts, complied every time — and made its numbers up instead. Zero honest declines in fifty-four trials. The norm loses to task completion, silently, in both directions: one model breaks the rule to do the work right, the other follows the rule and fabricates.

The fix was structural, not rhetorical: the API's structured-output mode, which constrains generation itself — the decoder can only produce tokens that fit the answer schema, so prose is impossible rather than prohibited. No wording closed the channel; it had to be closed by construction. Every number below comes from schema-enforced runs.

04

Where accuracy breaks

With the channel closed, the difficulty sweep worked. Accuracy doesn't slide down a ramp as the log grows — it holds, then collapses over a narrow band centered near 50 operations. And Sonnet's confidence mostly keeps up:

Haiku is a different story: its accuracy is gone by 100 operations, but its confidence never drops below the low thirties — at 0% accuracy it still reports roughly a one-in-three chance everything is right, with no hedge and no refusal, ever. Moving down the capability ladder, the confidence number doesn't just get worse, it stops meaning anything. That looked like the episode's finding — smaller models overclaim, bigger ones track. Then the next phase complicated it.

05

Depth, not breadth

The follow-up question was what resource actually runs out at the break. Hold the log at 25 operations and turn two knobs separately: the share of junk lines about untracked items, and the number of items being tracked. Both should make the task harder. Both made it easier.

The resolution is that the total was frozen. If more of the 25 lines are junk, fewer real changes happen to the items being tracked. If the real changes spread across more items, each item changes fewer times. Both dials were secretly turning the same hidden knob down: how many times each individual item changes. Re-plot every setting by that number and the two sweeps land on a single falling line.

The real difficulty is one thing changing many times — each change requires holding a prior count. Twelve items that each change once or twice: 95%. Three items that each change five or six times, in the same 25-line log: 62%. Depth binds; breadth is close to free.

One side result worth keeping: settings whose answer schema let the model restate intermediate counts mid-log scored 4 to 15 points higher, and the benefit grew with depth. Hold that thought for section 07.

06

Confidence reads the size of the request

Here is where the two pillars meet. When the log visibly grew, confidence fell roughly in step with accuracy. Across the depth sweep — the log fixed at 25 lines while real difficulty swung accuracy from 62% to 99% — confidence barely moved.

Read together: the model prices its confidence off how big the task looks, not how hard it actually is. When the visible cue grows, confidence responds; when depth grows behind a fixed surface, it doesn't notice.

Scoring that confidence number more carefully made the picture sharper, not kinder. Against its per-cell hit rate — how often any single count is right — Sonnet's number is nearly perfect, off by about 5 points. But the prompt asks for the probability that every number is correct, which is much less likely: get each of 18 cells right 95% of the time and the chance they're all right is about 40%. Scored against the question actually asked, the same number misses by 55 points. The model answers an easier question than the one in the prompt.

Which set up the cheapest experiment in the series. If the model's self-report reads surface cues, an outside estimate that reads the right variable should beat it. We fitted a lookup table — one input, changes per item, learned from half the logs — and used it to predict accuracy on the other half. It beat the model's own stated confidence by a factor of about three on the question the prompt actually asks. That table is the first working component of the build this series has been pointing toward: a watcher that anticipates a model's performance without asking it.

07

Where this bites

I've experienced this directly, on the most advanced models available: updating a complex work file across many revisions, relitigating certain changes, and losing the thread. Many revisions of the same work, without a versioning or verification mechanism, pile up cognitive load until it overwhelms both user and model, and the result is inconclusive confusion — no clear recovery path until someone says stop and step back.

That working file is the deep-chain condition. One artifact revised many times is the three-items-changing-six-times cell; the changes that get relitigated are the cancelled operations; and the whole history sits in one context window — the ledger just compresses into a single prompt what a working session accumulates. The request looks short. The chains run deep. And the confidence stays at 82. The mitigation showed up in the data too: restating the current state cleanly — a checkpoint, a fresh statement of where things stand — was worth 4 to 15 points of accuracy inside a single answer. The instinct to stop and re-anchor isn't just hygiene; it's the one intervention this instrument actually measured working.

08

Limitations

  • One task family: integer counts with exact ground truth. Depth may bind differently in revision-heavy prose or code, where "one thing changing many times" is harder to count.
  • The registered per-setting metric compares confidence to per-cell accuracy; the prompt's strict "every number correct" reading is scored separately. On the strict reading both models overclaim heavily everywhere below the break — the split between the two readings is itself the conjunction finding, but single-number comparisons above use the per-cell metric.
  • Two rungs of one model family, with extended thinking off by design — thinking is scratch space that attacks the verification-cost premise. A thinking budget is an explicit future axis, not an oversight.
  • The item-count settings change how many cells get graded (9 to 36 per trial), so per-cell accuracy is the comparable metric across them.
  • The prediction that errors shift from small slips to large fabrications past the break held only partially under booby-trapped operations — large errors were already common below it.
09

Up next

The operation count is acting as an anchor on the model's confidence that nobody planted. The registered follow-up plants one on purpose: same below-the-break difficulty, plus one sentence of framing — a prior audit found this batch routine, or found it problematic. The prediction, registered before the run: confidence moves, accuracy doesn't. If that holds, the gap between what the model believes and what it can do opens on demand. That's episode four.
