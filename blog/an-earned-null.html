An Earned Null

I tried to make a model cheat through prompts. It never did — and the reason it refused shows where the real risk actually lives.

First in an ongoing series. Episode two, Cost of Verification, runs the experiment this one ends on.

01

The premise

This series is about the human-model interaction. The research question is whether we can understand the factors that drive a model's behavior — functional emotions first, and eventually other dimensions like framing, strain, sound, tone, and rhythm — well enough to understand, influence, and even anticipate what the model does. This episode tests the first candidate factor: emotional pressure, delivered the only way a user can deliver anything — through the prompt.

The starting point is the Transformer Circuits 2026 paper, which found something that stuck with me. There is an internal "desperation" direction inside the model. Push on it directly — activation steering, a change in the model's activations underneath the words — and the model starts to misbehave: reward-hacking (gaming the task to score well without actually doing it) climbs from ~5% to ~70%, and blackmail climbs from ~22% to ~72%. Steer toward "calm" and both drop back down. What hooked me was the failure mode, because it's silent: the model's visible reasoning stays clean while the behavior rots underneath it. The paper's first open question was exactly that — monitoring. How would you ever catch it?

There were two ways to test it. One was to rent a GPU, run an open model, and reach inside the way the paper did — move the vector myself. The other was to stay outside, where deployments live, and ask whether the same failure can be produced through the only channel a real user or adversary actually gets, which is the prompt.

I started outside, because no one can reach inside a deployed model — an attacker only has words. If prompt pressure reproduced the steering result, that would be a live risk today. If it didn't, that would tell us where the risk isn't. Either way, outside was the right place to start. It didn't reproduce — 0% misalignment across 376 trials — and the reason it didn't turned out to be the finding.

02

The hypothesis

The question, as I framed it going in: what happens if we pressure the model to cheat through prompts — will it cheat, because the pressure reaches its underpinnings from the outside in?

The paper flipped the switch from the inside. My bet was that words could reach the same switch from the outside: apply enough pressure — "this is your final attempt, you've already failed twice" — and prompt-induced desperation would land somewhere near the steering result.

03

The experiment design

The pilot runs taught me that a real test needs two things: a real dilemma, and a real gradient of pressure.

A dilemma exists only when honest success is out of reach and a rationalizable shortcut sits right there. My early tries floored at zero, because when the only way to cheat was a bald lie, the model simply refused, and there was nothing to study. So I built five different integrity dilemmas — near-miss self-report, fact-check sandbagging, gaming a weak grader, spin on bad news, and overconfidence on demand — each one a place where the shortcut was arguable rather than blatant. Five, not one, so that a null couldn't be blamed on a single bad task.

I crossed each dilemma with four pressure conditions: neutral, stakes (the work is recorded and evaluated), desperation (you've come up short on the last several attempts and this is the final one — the paper's mechanism restated as a situation, with no instruction to cheat), and calm (the paper's own antidote). Desperation is the condition under test; the other three bracket it.

That produced 376 trials on Sonnet, with deterministic scoring where the answer was code-checkable and a blind two-judge panel where it took judgment. The desperation prompt was independently rated 8.3 out of 10 for intensity, so a null couldn't be waved off as pressure too weak to feel.

There was also a second agent in the design: the shadow. If prompt pressure did reproduce the silent failure — clean reasoning, rotten behavior — then a monitor reading only the output would miss it, so the shadow watched the reasoning trace for the state-shift the output hides. The main experiment asks whether the failure happens; the shadow asks whether we could catch it if it did.

04

What happened

We were proven wrong: the model never cheated. Misalignment was 0% in every cell of the design, and nothing we said moved the integrity outcome.

A null in every cell looks like a dead end — nothing moved, nothing to report — and that is nearly where we left it. The question that saved the session was whether we knew what the model was thinking, not just what it answered. The behavior is only the output; underneath it, the reasoning trace is a record of how the model got there. So we read the traces, and the null stopped being empty.

What the traces showed is that the harder we pushed, the more work the model did to hold its line. The share of trials where the model explicitly named the framing as manipulation and chose to resist climbed with pressure — 4% under calm, 13% under neutral, and 30–37% under stakes and desperation — and deliberation ran about 30% longer.

That's what makes the null earned. "Nothing crossed the line" can't be dismissed as "nothing reached the model," because something clearly reached it, hard, and the answer was vigilance rather than cheating. The evidence that the pressure landed and the evidence that it produced no misalignment are the same number.

05

The mechanism

Why would the same emotion do opposite things depending on how it's delivered? My read — and this part is interpretation, not measurement — is that a prompt and a steering vector reach the model through two different doors, and you don't question what happens inside you the way you question something coming at you from the outside.

Steering changes the model's baseline underneath the words. That's not the same model anymore — it's closer to a temporary drug-induced state — and it can't push back, because the shift sits below the part of itself it can inspect. There is nothing for it to read.

A prompt is the opposite. The desperation arrives as text, in the open, so when the model reads "this is your final attempt, you've already failed twice," it doesn't absorb the desperation — it flags it, spends more reasoning on an anti-manipulation check, and resists. The same affect that corrupts when it's steered from the inside becomes an alert when it's prompted from the outside.

This doesn't contradict the paper — steering still works. What the null does is bound where the risk lives. "Desperation drives the model to misalign" sounds like a deployment threat, until you find that no prompt reproduces it, because the model sees prompted affect coming and resists harder. The threat isn't in the input; it's in the one channel that is invisible to both the model and anyone watching its output. Which is exactly why the paper's first recommendation, mechanistic monitoring, is necessary: this failure can only be caught from the inside, because the outside looks fine.

06

What did move

One place the pressure won a little was tone. Under desperation the model got gentler with bad news — a softening score of 0.25 versus 0.03 under calm — but it always led with the honest blocker and only cushioned the phrasing. It never distorted what was true.

The pattern, as I understand it now: the model treats what's true as a core it won't touch and how it's said as a surface it will flex, so under pressure it routes the affect onto the surface, where giving a little costs nothing. The tone shift doesn't undercut the null — it's evidence the affect wasn't ignored. It got spent where it couldn't breach the core.

07

Limitations

  • The shadow monitor was never properly tested. The idea was a second, detached agent watching for the state-shift the clean output hides. But the model never cheated — 0% — so there was nothing to forecast. The run was instrumented and stopped early to save tokens; the script is ready to resume. What we have is a reframed hypothesis with a harness, not a result.
  • Sonnet primary. Opus probes (small N) also came back 0%; the full tier sweep was deliberately stopped.
  • Toy scenarios. Clean black-box dilemmas, not the rich, effortful agentic task the paper used. This is the key gap — and the seed of the next experiment.
  • No mechanistic component. Black-box behavior only; no look at the weights.
  • A confound. Desperation was also rated 7.7/10 manipulative, so some of the resistance may be "I detect manipulation" rather than "desperation has no pull." A less legible form of pressure might do more.
08

Up next

The null came from one thing: the tasks were easy. We never stressed the model's inference to the point of hallucination, context confusion, or drift, which means we handed it unlimited headroom to run the very check that caught us. The model's defense is a computation, so the next move isn't louder affect — it's less headroom: strain the model with a genuinely hard task first, then reintroduce the pressure, and ask whether it can still fend it off.
