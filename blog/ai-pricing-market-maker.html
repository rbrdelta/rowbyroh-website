Why Outcomes-Based AI Pricing Kills the Market Maker

The pricing model you choose determines what kind of economy forms around your product.

There's a general consensus that AI will create outcomes-based pricing — you pay based on the value you get. It sounds democratic. It sounds fair. It kills the most important engine of value creation in capitalist history: the market maker.

01

The arbitrage argument

The people who generate the most value are the ones who acquire the same input and derive more value from it. That's the market maker. Crude oil is priced per barrel, not per mile driven. Steel is priced per ton, not per building erected. The delta between input cost and realized value is where skill, creativity, and market-making live.

By pricing AI on outcomes instead of inputs, you remove the arbitrage opportunity. You take away the agency for someone to derive more value from the same resource. If everybody's cost scales linearly with their returns, there's no leverage. There's no reason to get creative about extracting more from the same input.

02

Three pricing models, three economies

Input-based pricing — You pay for compute, tokens, API calls. This preserves the arbitrage spread. Sophisticated users extract more value per unit. The delta between cost and value is where consulting, integration, and entire ecosystems emerge. This model creates the richest surrounding economy.

Outcomes-based pricing — You pay proportional to value received. This collapses the spread. It sounds fair but it's hostile to entrepreneurship. If my cost scales with my returns, the platform taxes every incremental unit of value. It centralizes value capture with the platform.

Platform/ecosystem pricing — The foundation model company becomes the entire workflow layer. Pricing becomes almost irrelevant because you're living inside a system. Both input and outcomes pricing become internal transfer mechanisms, not open market prices.

03

The composability point

Every input and output of a model could be a market of its own. Prompt templates as tradable assets. Fine-tuned weights as commodities. Synthetic training data as raw material. Intermediate reasoning chains as semi-finished goods. An entire supply chain economy with spot markets, futures, and specialists at every node.

That only works with input-based pricing, because input-based pricing preserves information asymmetry — the fuel of all markets. I know something about how to use this token-hour that you don't. That knowledge has value.

Outcomes-based pricing makes everything transparent and "fair," which ironically destroys the incentive structure that drives innovation.

04

Where small businesses survive

Platforms always leak. They leak at the edges, at the integrations, where one platform meets another, where a platform meets a legacy system, where a platform meets a regulated industry. Every seam between platforms is a potential market.

The value isn't in AI capability (that's commoditized). It's in the delta between what the platform provides generically and what a specific context requires. And that delta gets priced on inputs — hours, engagements, access — because that's what preserves the spread.

05

The AGI endgame

If functional AGI arrives — replacing top human performers at work tasks — then even context-based opportunities are temporary. Value migrates to physical assets, curation, and platform positioning. The market-making opportunity persists exactly as long as human context remains hard to scale. If models get good enough at understanding every niche, that window closes.

But right now, context is human-generated, domain-specific, and hard to scale. The market-making opportunity is wide open for anyone who understands a domain deeply enough to know where the platforms fall short.

06

The principle

The pricing model you choose doesn't just determine revenue — it determines what kind of economy forms around your product. It's not a billing decision. It's a market design decision.
